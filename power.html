Power + Batteries

The spacecraft that we launch require electrical power. Unfortunately, regular double A batteries simply don’t cut it for most missions.



Since the start of space exploration, humanity has been developing and testing increasingly durable and powerful battery technology in space. The operations of these batteries are crucial to the success of any mission, since they power important systems on the spacecraft including navigation, communication, and astronaut life support.

Over the past 60 years, many different batteries have been used, each with their own benefits and drawbacks. The most commonly used batteries have been nickel cadmium, nickel hydrogen, and lithium ion. These batteries have to be extremely rugged, able to withstand the incredible pressures at launch, as well as operate over a wide temperature range in the vacuum of space.

Nickel Cadmium Batteries

One of the earlier batteries used in space, nickel cadmium batteries have been a staple of space missions since 1959. The first NiCd battery was used on the Explorer 6, which took some of the first photos of earth from space. The battery continued in use on many weather satellites, orbiting the earth.

The operation of a nickel cadmium battery requires the knowledge of some basic electrochemistry. It operates as an electrolytic cell, with a positively charged electrode of nickel (III) oxide-hydroxide and a negatively charged electrode of cadmium. These are separated by an electrolyte, commonly potassium hydroxide.

At the anode, cadmium combines with hydroxide ions to form cadmium hydroxide and release two electrons:

Cd + 2OH- → Cd(OH)2 + 2e-

At the cathode, nickel (III) oxide-hydroxide reacts with water to form nickel hydroxide and hydroxide ions. This reaction consumes two electrons:

2NiOOH + H2O + 2e- → Ni(OH)2 + 2OH-

The complete balanced equation for a nickel cadmium battery is:

2NiOOH + H2O + Cd → Cd(OH)2 + Ni(OH)2

The current formed by the moving electrons powers all the instruments and systems onboard the spacecraft. Nickel cadmium batteries are rare in modern spacecraft, but they were the prime choice for missions in the 60s. They were used because nickel cadmium batteries are able to withstand rough situations and cold temperatures, making them ideal for spacecraft. In addition to this, they are more energy dense when compared to the other batteries at the time

Unfortunately, there are a couple drawbacks. Cadmium can be fairly expensive, because of its rarity. Also, a nickel cadmium battery requires an intricate setup to charge, which adds to possible complications during flight.

Nickel Hydrogen Batteries

Nickel hydrogen batteries are the successor of nickel cadmium batteries. They were first used as a backup to the NiCd batteries during a space flight, but were the sole power source for the intelsat V in 1983. Nowadays, NiH2 batteries are used in satellites, primarily satellites in geostationary orbit. Nickel hydrogen batteries have many notable uses. They’re used in the Hubble Space Telescope and were used to power the international space station

ckel hydrogen batteries use the same cathode as nickel cadmium batteries, nickel (III) oxide-hydroxide, and the same anode as a fuel cell, hydrogen gas.

In a fuel cell reaction, the hydrogen loses an electron, creating a hydrogen ion. The ion passes through the electrolyte while the electrons pass through a wire, creating a current. The electron is then used in a reaction to create H2O.

A nickel hydrogen battery uses hydrogen gas in the same way:

½H2 + OH- → H2O + e-

The nickel (III) oxide-hydroxide combines with water and an electron to create nickel hydroxide and a hydroxide ion:

NiOOH + H2O + e- → Ni(OH)2 + OH-

The complete balanced reaction for a nickel hydrogen battery is:

NiOOH + ½H2 → Ni(OH)2

Nickel hydrogen batteries combine these two existing technologies to create a battery worthwhile of space travel. These batteries have a long life and are able to charge and discharge many times without degrading. Unfortunately, nickel hydrogen requires battery conditioning if it doesn’t completely charge and recharge on a single cycle.

Lithium Ion Batteries

Lithium ion batteries are extremely common. You probably have one in the device that you’re using to read this. These batteries are being used in many spacecraft for the same reasons that device manufacturers are using them. Lithium ion batteries are lighter and smaller than many other kinds of batteries, including nickel hydrogen. They also are much more energy efficient and easy to access, thanks to their commonality in consumer devices.

Unfortunately, they do have a tendency to explode or catch fire. (Remember Samsung’s Galaxy Note 7?) Considering using these on a spacecraft has required space agencies to perform many tests and develop additional safety features for the lithium ion batteries used on space ships.

Lithium ion batteries use lithium-cobalt oxide as a positive electrode and graphite as a negative electrode. When the battery is charging, the positive electrode releases a lithium ion along with its electron. The lithium ion moves through the electrolyte, while the electrons move through the wire, meeting at the graphite. When discharging the battery, the electrons move back through the wire and the lithium ions move back through the electrolyte. The flow of ions and electrons are interconnected, one will not cross without the other. When the battery is disconnected from whatever it is powering, the electrons will stop, stopping the flow of lithium ions as well.

As the reign of nickel hydrogen batteries comes to an end, the lithium ion batteries have become the next giant in spacecraft power sources and will likely continue to be used until a better battery is created. As recently as January of 2017, all of the nickel hydrogen batteries on the ISS were replaced with lithium ion batteries, including the smaller batteries powering space suits!
