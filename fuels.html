Rocket Propellants

Rockets use propellants, fuels, and oxidizers.

A propellant is made up of a fuel and an oxidizer and is the chemical mixture burned to generate thrust in rockets. Propellants are classified according to their state: liquid, solid, or hybrid. A Fuel is a substance that burns when combined with oxygen-producing gas for propulsion. An oxidizer is an agent that releases oxygen in combination with a fuel. For fuel to burn in space where no air exists, an oxidizer is necessary.

The mixture ratio is the ratio of oxidizer to fuel.



Most rocket engines use chemical propellants. There are many types, such as liquid propellants, solid propellants, gelled propellants, hybrid propellants, or gaseous propellants, depending on their physical state. Most modern rockets use a combination of both solid and liquid fuel boosters. For the initial launch sequence, solid fuels are used because the speed needs to be at its maximum. Later on, liquid fuels are used so that the speed may be adjusted to get the rocket’s payload on to the right trajectory.



The efficiency of rocket propellants is rated using specific impulse. Specific impulse is the change in momentum per unit mass (measured in pounds or kilograms) for rocket fuels. Specific impulse will vary depending on the type of propellant; however, its exact value will vary to some extent with the rocket engine's operating conditions and design.

Solid Propellants

Solid propellants are comprised of a mixture of fuel and oxidizer compounds already combined. Solid propellant motors consist of a casing, usually steel, filled with solid propellants that burn rapidly. Hot gases are expelled from a nozzle to produce thrust. A spark is all that is needed to start them burning. Once ignited a solid propellant burns from the center out towards the sides of the casing. The burn rate and pattern are dependent on the shape of the center channel, thus providing a means to control thrust. Once ignited, they cannot be stopped and will burn until all the propellant is exhausted. There are two types of solid propellant: homogeneous propellant and composite propellant.

Homogeneous propellants come in 2 varieties.

Single base propellants consist of a single compound, usually nitrocellulose ((C6H7(NO2)3O5)n). Single base propellants have both an oxidation capacity and a reduction capacity.

Double base propllants consist of nitrocellulose and a secondary combustible, to which a plasticizer is added.Double base propellants do not produce traceable fumes and are commonly used in tactical weapons. Under normal conditions, homogeneous propellants do not usually have specific impulses greater than about 210.

Modern composite propellants are made up of heterogeneous powders (mixtures) that use a crystallized or finely ground mineral salt as an oxidizer. The oxidizer used is often ammonium perchlorate (NH4ClO4), and the fuel is aluminum. A polymeric binder (such as polyurethane or polybutadienes) holds the propellant together and is also consumed as fuel.

Sometimes, a catalyst such as an iron compound is added to help increase the burning rate. Composite propellants are often identified by the polymeric binder used. Two types of binders commonly used are polybutadiene acrylonitrile (PBAN) and hydroxy-terminator polybutadiene (HTPB).

The benefits of using PBAN formulations are that they have a slightly higher specific impulse, density, and burn rate than equivalent formulations using HTPB. The problem with PBAN propellant is that it is more challenging to mix and process and requires an elevated curing temperature. On the other hand, HTPB binder is stronger and more flexible than PBAN binder. Both PBAN and HTPB formulations result in propellants that deliver excellent performance, have good mechanical properties, and offer potentially long burn times.

Liquid Propellants

A liquid propellant is used in a liquid-propellant rocket engine. In a liquid propellant rocket, the fuel and oxidizer are stored in separate tanks, and are combined in a combustion chamber where they are burned to produce thrust.

An excellent liquid propellant is one with a high specific impulse. This means it has a high combustion temperature and exhaust gases with small molecular weights. Another critical factor is the density of the propellant. Lower density propellants require larger storage tanks, thus increasing the mass of the launch vehicle. As well, storage temperature is essential.

Petroleum fuels are highly refined forms of crude oil and are a mixture of complex hydrocarbons. The petroleum used as rocket fuel is highly refined kerosene mixed with liquid oxygen. Petroleum fuels are used as the propellant in the first-stage boosters of the Atlas and Delta II launch vehicles. Petroleum fuels were also used to power the first stages of the Saturn 1B and Saturn V rockets.

Hypergolic propellants are fuels and oxidizers that combust on contact with each other and require no ignition source. These fuels only need nitric acid to ignite, and therefore, the easy start and restart capability of hypergols make them ideal for spacecraft maneuvering systems. This spontaneity allows for an incredible degree of control over the thrust that boosters using hypergolic propellants supply. Also, hypergols remain liquid at normal temperatures and therefore do not have the storage problems of cryogenic propellants. Hypergols are highly toxic and must be handled with extreme care.

Common hypergolic fuels include hydrazine, monomethylhydrazine (MMH), unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine (UDMH). There are also blended fuels such as Aerozine 50, which is a combination of 50% UDMH and 50% hydrazine. Aerozine 50 + nitrogen tetroxide (NTO) was widely used in historical American rockets, including the Titan II, all engines in the Apollo Lunar Module, and the service propulsion system in the Apollo service module.

Cryogenic propellants are liquefied gases stored at incredibly cold temperatures. Due to the low temperatures of cryogenic propellants, they are difficult to store over long periods. Cryogenic propellants do not spontaneously ignite. When reaction time and stability are not concerns, their high efficiency makes these very effective propellants.

Rocket Fuel Over Time

Since the beginning of spaceflight, there have not been many changes in the chemistry of rocket fuel. However, scientists are busy trying to find ways to design more fuel- efficient rockets. Space launches can have a hefty carbon footprint due to the burning of solid rocket fuels. Obviously, to improve their efficiency, rockets need to be less fuel hungry. This means the fuel needs to be released out the back as fast as possible to give the desired momentum and achieve the same thrust. One possibility is to use ionized gases by propelling them through a rocket nozzle using a magnetic accelerator. Ionized gas weighs much less than traditional rocket fuels. The ionized particles are pushed out the back of the rocket at an incredibly high velocity, which compensates for their small weight or mass. Another possibility is the use of hydrogen peroxide. Some scientists believe it could be the critical element in a new class of eco-friendly rocket propellants and highly efficient fuel cells. Hydrogen peroxide for rocket propulsion would be made up of 3 percent water and 97 percent hydrogen peroxide with critical contaminants removed. This propulsion system could be an inexpensive alternative to non-renewable hydrocarbon fuels.
